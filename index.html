FAQ

Questions I get every season.

Straight answers. No jargon.

I drove for Uber this year and got a 1099. It says I made $38K — but I didn't keep any of it. Do I pay taxes on that number?

+

No. The 1099 shows gross earnings, but you're taxed on profit — after deducting mileage, phone, insurance, and other real business costs. Most rideshare and delivery drivers end up owing far less than that top-line number suggests. I track down every deduction you're legally owed and make sure the IRS only sees what's actually taxable.

I haven't filed in two years. How bad is it?

+

Probably less bad than you think — but the longer you wait, the more penalties stack up. I handle unfiled returns regularly. We figure out what you actually owe (sometimes it's less than feared, sometimes there's even a refund), file everything correctly, and get you back in good standing with the IRS. The first step is just finding out where you stand.

My employer pays me as a 1099 contractor, but I work there full-time. Does that affect my taxes?

+

Yes — significantly. As a 1099 worker, you're responsible for both halves of Social Security and Medicare (15.3%), and nothing is withheld from your checks. I see this situation constantly. We make sure you're claiming the right deductions, set up quarterly estimated payments so there's no surprise bill in April, and review whether your classification is even accurate.

I got an IRS letter saying I owe money I don't recognize. What do I do?

+

Don't ignore it, but don't panic either. Most IRS notices are routine — a missing form, a math discrepancy, or a matching issue where income was reported to the IRS that wasn't on your return. I review the notice, tell you in plain English what it actually means, and handle the response. I deal with these letters regularly — they're almost always solvable.

My small business turned a profit this year for the first time. How much should I be setting aside?

+

A rough starting point is 25–30% of net profit for federal and state taxes combined, but the real number depends on your structure, deductions, and other income. I work with new business owners to calculate actual estimated tax payments — quarterly — so you're not blindsided at year-end. Getting this right in year one saves a lot of pain later.

I'm not a US citizen but I earned income here. Do I need to file?

+

Yes, in most cases. Non-residents with US-source income file a 1040-NR, and the rules around what's taxable depend on your visa type and how long you were in the country. If you don't have an SSN, I also handle ITIN applications. This is an area where errors are common and the consequences are real — I make sure it's done right.
